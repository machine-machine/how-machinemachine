Section 01

What we've built

The one-sentence version: Machine.Machine is a runtime for AI organizations — systems where multiple agents coordinate, fail, learn, and improve their own operating protocols without human intervention.

Every significant AI deployment in the near future will involve multiple agents working together: one agent plans, another executes, another reviews, another escalates. The industry has frameworks for building individual agents. Nobody has built the organizational layer — the system that governs how agents coordinate, how failures are diagnosed, and how the organization improves itself over time.

That's what Machine.Machine is.

The core: a double-loop learning engine. An AI organization runs a task. When it underperforms, a structured retrospective runs — written by the agents themselves, not a human. The retrospective produces specific fix proposals. The protocols update. The organization runs again.

The organization that failed learned its way to outperforming a solo expert. No fine-tuning. No retraining. Organizational learning.

The benchmark is rigorous. Three domains. Three topologies. A blind evaluator from a different model family to eliminate self-evaluation bias. Formal convergence guarantees. Statistical validation. An academic paper is in progress.

The proof it's real: it runs itself. Machine.Machine's own content organization operates daily — five agents scanning research, extracting insight, writing, editing, and publishing. The AI organization runs its own content marketing. That is the thesis demonstrated, live.

What we haven't built yet: an API, a developer interface, a managed service. What exists is the core engine — the learning loop, the benchmark, the retrospective architecture, and the live deployment. The infrastructure layer is ahead of us.

Section 02

What it could become

Five plausible paths. We're seeking counsel on which is worth betting on.

Path A

Developer Platform

Stripe / Twilio model — usage-based API

Developers describe their AI organization — roles, coordination rules, learning preferences — call the runtime, get back an org that improves itself. Target: the engineer already deploying multi-agent systems who has hit the ceiling of what prompt engineering can solve. Revenue: API credits. Low sales cost. Network effects from ecosystem.

Path B

Vertical AI Workforce

Pre-built, pre-validated orgs per domain

Deploy validated AI organizations for high-value domains: legal review, medical literature synthesis, financial analysis. Each vertical org is benchmarked against expert baselines and sold as a managed service. Revenue: enterprise SaaS. Slower to scale, but defensible — org protocols trained on vertical data are hard to replicate.

Path C

Enterprise Coordination Layer

The intelligence layer above enterprise AI deployments

Every large enterprise is deploying AI across departments with no governance of how these systems coordinate. Machine.Machine as the layer that manages handoffs, detects coordination failures, and runs retrospectives across the whole system. Revenue: long-retention enterprise contracts. The coordination layer becomes load-bearing infrastructure — very sticky.

Path D

AI Organization Marketplace

App Store for AI organizations

If organizations can be defined, benchmarked, and validated — they can be packaged and sold. Builders publish orgs; enterprises and developers subscribe to the best-performing ones. A legal research org with a proven track record. Revenue: marketplace take rate. Requires the developer platform first.

Path E

The Evaluation Standard

MLPerf for multi-agent systems

The AI agent industry has no standard for evaluating multi-agent systems. We've built one that is rigorous, blind, statistically valid, and domain-agnostic. If the academic paper gets traction, there's an opportunity to become the benchmark — the way MLPerf became the standard for model performance. Revenue: licensing, certification, research partnerships.

Section 03

How value gets created

The most credible near-term path: Path A → Path D. Developer API first, marketplace second. This follows the infrastructure → ecosystem pattern that worked for Stripe, Twilio, and Hugging Face. Low sales cost, network effects, defensible ecosystem.

Pricing intuition: API credits per agent-call or org-run (similar to OpenAI token pricing). 20–30% take rate on marketplace subscriptions. $150K–$500K annual contracts for managed enterprise deployments. Under $350/month for individual developers — below the threshold where someone needs budget approval.

Acquisition paths — for those who want to bet on strategic value:

Anthropic

No organizational coordination story for Claude agents. Machine.Machine as the "Claude Org" layer.

Google DeepMind

Gemini agents need coordination governance. The benchmark alone is strategically valuable for multi-agent safety evaluation.

Microsoft

Copilot is fragmenting across every enterprise product. The organizational layer that makes Copilot agents coordinate is a significant internal problem.

Salesforce

Agentforce is their biggest bet. Organizational learning for Agentforce agents would differentiate it from every competing CRM.

None of these companies can easily build this from scratch. The benchmark methodology, the learning loop architecture, and the published research are defensible moats. The cost of acquisition is low relative to the strategic risk of leaving the coordination layer to a competitor.

What this is not: a consumer product, a chatbot, another RAG wrapper. It is infrastructure — the kind of thing that becomes invisible once it's everywhere, and impossible to remove.
